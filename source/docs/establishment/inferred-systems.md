# Reconstructed from the receipts

The Establishment does not publish its estate. There is no inventory, no architecture
diagram on any wall an outside reader reaches, no list of what runs where. What there is, is
a paper trail that was never meant to be read as one: procurement notices, the four-yearly
defence vision document, the standing collaboration arrangements with the University and the
Guilds, the budget line under continuity of civic function, the occasional supplier dispute
that reaches the courts. None of it describes a system. Read together, it implies one.

So this page is a reconstruction, not a record. It combines three things: what the public
traces show, what any organisation of this size tends to build because the patterns are by
now ordinary, and the constraints any body that handles classified material carries whatever
city it sits in. The result is a reference picture that is plausible and usual for this kind
of institution. It is not a claim that this runs exactly there. The hand is inferred from the
fingerprints; the estate is inferred from the receipts. The gaps in the picture are the part
worth attention, because they mark where the trail was meant to go cold.

## The ladder, briefly

Defence estates are organised less by technology than by classification, and the technology
follows. The Establishment uses a ladder that maps onto the city's own signals.

At the open end sits everything that can be lost without harm: the public-facing name and its
notices, recruitment material, the annual report once it is out. This lives where anyone's
ordinary documents now live, on rented compute, treated as cloud has come to be treated, as a
default rather than a decision.

A step-up is internal but low-harm: scheduling, Guild rates and secondment records, the
logistics of who is where. Restricted is the next rung, where a leak could carry operational
weight without being catastrophic: procedures, system descriptions, the non-urgent reading of
clacks traffic and semaphore patterns. Confidential is where the Quiet Room's characterised
intercepts begin to live, and the reading of who could darken a Grand Trunk tower.

At the sealed end is the smallest tier and the most guarded: the Long Table's consolidated
view before it reaches the Patrician, the signal-readers' rawest material, the firmware work
the University conducts under academic loan. This tier does not sit on anyone's rented
compute. It sits behind the city's own walls, or the University's, and the difference between
those two is itself a thing the documentation does not settle.

Between the open end and the sealed end is a broad grey middle, and most of the interesting
arguments happen there.

## Golem Trust Computing

The rented compute, at the lower rungs, comes from [Golem Trust Computing](../org/index.rst). It is the obvious
supplier and very nearly the only one at scale, which is the first thing a reconstruction
notices: a single provider carrying most of the routine load is a dependency before it is a
convenience.

A golem works because of the word in its head. Whoever holds the word holds the golem. This
turns out to be the whole of the sovereignty question, stated plainly for once. The usual
three axes, who holds the keys, who can reach the machine, and where the thing that issues
instructions actually sits, collapse onto a single object you can point at. Who holds the
golem's word. Who is permitted to speak to it. Whose Trust runs the yard it stands in.

The nuance that the public discussion most often misses is that location is not control. A
golem standing in a yard inside the city is still a Trust golem; the word in its head was
written under the Trust's arrangements, and the Trust can in principle read it. Compute that
sits physically nearby can still answer, in the end, to a governance the city does not own.
This is why the periodic announcements of a sovereign arrangement are less about moving work
off Golem Trust Computing and more about who gets to hold the word once it is there: the
keys kept locally, the logs kept locally, the right to speak to the golem narrowed to people
the city has chosen. The compute barely moves. The custody does.

The sealed tier resolves this by not using Trust golems at all, or by using ones whose words
never leave a room the city controls. That is expensive, which is why the tier is small, and
why the pressure to push work down the ladder into the rented yard is constant.

## The bridges

The estate is not really a stack of layers. It is a set of separated worlds that talk to
each other through a small number of controlled crossings, and the crossings are the actual
design. Anyone can buy compute. The work, and the cost, is in the bridges.

A bridge between two classification worlds is built to be difficult on purpose. Material moves
upward more freely than down: a lower world can hand something to a higher one, often
aggregated or stripped of detail on the way. Downward movement is the guarded direction.
Material rarely descends without being filtered, downgraded, or rewritten into a form that is
safe to hold at the lower level, and some of it never descends at all. Where the requirement
is absolute, the crossing is a one-way device by construction, a diode that physically cannot
carry a reply. Every transfer is logged, and the log is kept somewhere the people doing the
transferring cannot quietly amend.

This is where the reconstruction meets the rest of the institution, because the bridges are
the attack surface, and in the same precise sense the [about page](about.md) means it. The
Establishment's exposure was never mainly in its compute. It is in the crossings, where one
world's account of events becomes another world's input. An adversary who shapes what passes
a bridge, the sanitised summary that goes up, the downgraded picture that comes down, shapes
what the higher world believes without touching anything operational. It is the same gap as
the interpretation stage, rendered as plumbing. The summary that crosses is an interpretation.
Whoever owns the crossing owns the belief on the far side of it.

So the governance arguments, when they are honest, are rarely about the machines. They are
about the bridges: what is allowed to cross, in which direction, who signs for it, and whether
the log can be trusted.

## The alliance and the ally

The classification ladder is not the city's invention. It comes, in its shape and most of its
vocabulary, from the mutual arrangement the city keeps with the other powers around the Circle
Sea, a standing understanding about defence that everyone treats as binding and no one quite
calls a treaty. The arrangement supplies the shared scheme, which is what lets material cross
between allies at all: a thing marked restricted in one capital is meant to be held as
restricted in the next.

Fungolia is the closest of these allies, and the most watched. The arrangement is real and the
sharing is real; the Fungolian observer post that signs nothing and attends everything is, on
paper, a partner with standing. The standing question, the one no document frames, is the same
as ever. The shared scheme means Fungolia can hold the city's restricted material by right.
Which of it the post then reads more closely than holding requires, and which capital reads the
post's letters home, is not a question the arrangement was built to answer. The closest ally is
the one with the most legitimate access, which is exactly why it is the one worth watching.

## What the gaps say

A reconstruction like this is only as good as its honesty about where it stops. The ladder is
inferable, the reliance on a single provider is inferable, the existence of the bridges is
inferable because an estate this shape cannot work without them. What stays dark is the sealed
tier, the wording of the actual crossing rules, and the list of who may sign for a downward
transfer. That darkness is not an oversight. It is the design holding.

The estate, in the end, is not a place. It is a series of worlds with controlled crossings
between them, and the city's real defensive engineering is spent on the crossings, not the
compute. Which is the same thing the rest of these pages keep saying, in a different language:
the structure is not in what is held, but in what is allowed to pass.
